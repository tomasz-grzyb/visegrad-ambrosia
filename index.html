Project Overview

Duration June 2026 - November 2027
Budget €15,000
Countries Poland, Slovakia, Hungary, Bosnia and Herzegovina
The Problem: Common ragweed (Ambrosia artemisiifolia) is one of Central Europe's most destructive invasive plants, causing up to €7.4 billion in European healthcare costs and reducing crop yields by up to 50% in affected areas.

Project Description

Our international research team will investigate how fungi (mycobiome) and bioactive compounds (metabolites) contribute to the extraordinary invasiveness of common ragweed across Central Europe. Using cutting-edge molecular and chemical analyses, we will sample 12 strategic locations across the V4+ region to identify patterns that could reveal new biocontrol strategies.

Why This Research Matters

Ragweed affects up to 40% of people in heavily invaded areas, with models predicting sensitization could increase from 33 to 77 million Europeans by 2041-60. In Hungary alone, ragweed consumes approximately 1% of GDP through combined healthcare and agricultural losses. This project aims to understand the molecular mechanisms behind ragweed's success to develop sustainable control methods.

Key Research Questions

  • Which fungi are consistently associated with ragweed across its invasion range?
  • What chemical compounds give ragweed its competitive advantage?
  • Can we identify natural compounds that limit ragweed's own growth (autotoxicity)?
  • How do these patterns vary across the invasion gradient from epicenter to expanding frontiers?

Research Methodology

Mycobiome Analysis

Comprehensive molecular characterization of ragweed's fungal communities (both surface and internal) using DNA sequencing and qPCR quantification across all sampling sites.

Chemical Profiling

Advanced GC×GC-MS analysis to identify and quantify terpenoids and phenolic acids from various ragweed tissues, creating complete metabolomic profiles.

Autotoxicity Testing

Germination assays using ragweed extracts to identify compounds that may limit the plant's own growth, potentially revealing natural bioherbicide candidates.

Strategic Sampling

12 locations across 4 countries: 6 sites in the Pannonian Basin invasion epicenter (Hungary/Slovakia) and 6 from expanding frontiers (Poland/Bosnia).

This project pioneers "invasive biotechnology" - a systematic approach to understanding invasion mechanisms at the molecular level.

Expected Impact

Scientific Contributions

  • First comprehensive mycobiome map: Revealing fungal associations across Central European invasion gradient
  • Complete metabolomic profiles: Identifying chemical compounds underlying ragweed's competitive success
  • Biocontrol potential: Discovering natural enemies or autotoxic compounds for sustainable management
  • Methodological framework: Establishing standardized protocols for invasive species molecular ecology
